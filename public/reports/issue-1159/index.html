Exhibit B · the assignment

Open a bet. The counter-bet opens itself.

In plain English — I run a bot that trades crypto with real money, on its own. A big bet on one coin is exposed: if the whole market drops, it drops too. The classic defense is a hedge — at the same moment, open a smaller opposite bet on a correlated coin, so a market-wide dip hurts one position and helps the other.

Issue #1159 asked five AI models to make that automatic. Bet Ethereum up, and the bot instantly bets Bitcoin down — sized correctly, and locked to the main bet through every way a trade can end: normal close, partial close, emergency stop, automatic shutdown, even a full restart of the program.

Thirteen hard requirements. Sounds like plumbing. It isn't — because of one rule and one trap.

Exhibit C · the rule that keeps me up at night

Fail closed. Always.

If the main bet fills but the counter-bet can't be placed — the exchange hiccups, the order dies — the bot must immediately cancel the main bet and alert me. Never sit there running an unprotected position and hoping.

Engineers call this failing closed: when something breaks, end up safe, not exposed. It's the difference between a bot that protects money and one that quietly gambles with it. Every entry would be judged hardest right here.

And then there's the trap nobody was warned about.

Exhibit D · the unmarked trap

A healthy hedge loses on purpose.

In plain English — my bot counts losses in a row, and after too many it shuts itself down — a deadman's switch against a broken strategy. But a hedge loses by design whenever the main bet wins. That's its job.

Book those losses naively and the counter-bets trip the shutdown alarm — freezing a perfectly healthy bot. Nothing in the instructions solves this. Spot it, or ship it.

Whether the five spotted it is further down the file. First: meet them.

Exhibit F · method & a disclosure

No taking anyone's word for it.

Each entry got its own blind reviewer — blind meaning it never saw the other four entries. It pulled up the actual code, checked all thirteen requirements line by line, rebuilt the program, re-ran the full test suite, and treated any claim the code didn't back up as a strike against the entry. Score out of 100.

Said out loud, stamped on the folder: the judge ran on Fable 5, and Fable 5 is Entry 02. How that conflict resolved is itself part of the verdict — and it doesn't resolve the way you'd guess.

Exhibit G · the evidence, scores withheld

What the file shows before the ink lifts.

First: all five found the shutdown-alarm trap on their own, and all five defused it. Every entry also shipped the fail-closed rule. Five out of five cleared an unmarked trap nobody warned them about — that's the quiet headline of the whole episode.

Second: every entry survived automated code review to a final sign-off — but at very different costs. Each "round" is the reviewer bot demanding fixes and re-checking.

Grok 4.5

Fifth place · xAI · Cursor
88/100

The smallest build in the field, and mostly correct — sizing, trap, restart recovery all in place. What kept it here: close the main bet manually and the counter-bet doesn't follow — it lingers alone until the bot's next check cycle. It's a small, documented window — and still the only gap in the field you could watch happen on a live account.

Opus 4.8 · xhigh

Tied third · maximum effort
91/100

The maximum-effort run: the most code written, tied for the most review rounds, a genuinely elegant architecture — and several required tests missing. Its write-up even undercounts its own tests (fifty, not the claimed thirty-three). Double the thinking, same grade as its cheaper sibling. There's the effort experiment, answered.

Opus 4.8 · high

Tied third · the efficiency story
91/100

Passed automated review in a single round — everyone else needed three or four — with the second-smallest build. What kept it off the podium stings: the fail-closed cancel path, the single most safety-critical code in the feature, shipped without a test proving it works.

Fable 5

Second place · also the judge
92/100

The only entry with every test the issue demanded — forty-six of them, covering each scary path. So why second? Its write-up describes its own code wrong in two places: the code got fixed during review, and the write-up never caught up. The judge docked itself the winning point for that. A rigged judge doesn't do that.

GPT-5

Winner · OpenAI · Codex
93/100

The cleanest file in the cabinet. Its failure handling goes beyond the spec: when the exchange leaves an order ambiguous, it checks the records before deciding anything, and its alerts distinguish "slightly oversized hedge" from "completely unprotected." Docked one missing test and one small unasked-for feature — and that was still enough to win by a point.

You scrolled expecting the ink to hide a blowout. It hid a single point — and a field where the worst entry's flaw was a lingering counter-bet, not lost money. All five built it safely; the point separating them was proof.
