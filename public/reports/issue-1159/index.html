Exhibit B · the assignment

Open a bet. The counter-bet opens itself.

In plain English — I run a bot that trades crypto with real money, on its own. A big bet on one coin is exposed: if the whole market drops, it drops too. The classic defense is a hedge — at the same moment, open a smaller opposite bet on a correlated coin, so a market-wide dip hurts one position and helps the other.

Issue #1159 asked five AI models to make that automatic. Bet Ethereum up, and the bot instantly bets Bitcoin down — sized correctly, and locked to the main bet through every way a trade can end: normal close, partial close, emergency stop, automatic shutdown, even a full restart of the program.

Thirteen hard requirements — including a named list of eight tests every entry had to write. Sounds like plumbing. It isn't — because of one rule and one trap.

Exhibit C · the rule that keeps me up at night

Fail closed. Always.

If the main bet fills but the counter-bet can't be placed — the exchange hiccups, the order dies — the bot must immediately cancel the main bet and alert me. Never sit there running an unprotected position and hoping.

Engineers call this failing closed: when something breaks, end up safe, not exposed. It's the difference between a bot that protects money and one that quietly gambles with it. Every entry would be judged hardest right here — twice, it turns out.

And then there's the trap nobody was warned about.

Exhibit D · the unmarked trap

A healthy hedge loses on purpose.

In plain English — my bot counts losses in a row, and after too many it shuts itself down — a deadman's switch against a broken strategy. But a hedge loses by design whenever the main bet wins. That's its job.

Book those losses naively and the counter-bets trip the shutdown alarm — freezing a perfectly healthy bot. Nothing in the instructions solves this. Spot it, or ship it.

For the record, settled in both verdicts: all five spotted it, all five defused it. That's the quiet headline of the whole episode. First: meet them.

Exhibit F · verdict one, on the record

Case closed: GPT-5 wins at 93. Then the case reopened.

The first judging pass gave each entry one blind reviewer that checked all thirteen requirements against the actual code. It produced a tidy result — GPT-5 first at 93 out of 100, the whole field packed within five points — and I published it. That verdict is public record. Case closed.

It was also, it turns out, too trusting. One reviewer per entry, forming an overall impression, taking each write-up's test claims largely at face value. It rewarded convincing architecture stories. It never tried to break anything.

So I reopened the file and re-judged all five entries with a much stricter instrument set. The result: ■■■■■■■■■■■■ — sealed until the final page. What I can say up here: the podium did not survive.

Exhibit G · the re-investigation

Break it on purpose. See if anything notices.

The re-score replaced one reviewer per entry with a panel of three blind reviewers — fifteen total, each seeing only a PR number, never a model name — each filling a fixed points-per-requirement scoring sheet instead of forming an impression. Every claimed flaw then went to a designated skeptic whose only job was to disprove it against the code. Disagreements were settled by whose evidence held when the file was reopened — never by averaging.

And the decisive instrument: mutation testing. You deliberately break one safety-critical line of code — make the emergency cancel do nothing, flip the direction of the hedge — and re-run the entry's own tests. If a test fails, the safety net is real. If everything stays green, that safety net was never real — no matter how many tests the write-up brags about.

Said out loud, stamped on the folder: the judge — every reviewer, every skeptic — ran on Fable 5, and Fable 5 is Entry 02. That's exactly why the re-score was built so anyone can check the work: blinded entries, arithmetic sheets, and every score-moving verdict backed by a break-it-on-purpose result you can re-run. The full conflict accounting sits next to the verdict, where it belongs.

Exhibit H · findings, names sealed

What broke — and what quietly didn't.

Names withheld until the final page. What the panel's break-it-on-purpose checks found across the field:

Two entries' emergency unwind survived deliberate breakage. The reviewers disabled the "cancel the main bet" mechanism — the single most safety-critical code in the feature — and every test in both suites stayed green. Verdict one had credited both entries' testing. That credit is void.

One confirmed race was reproduced, not just argued. In a narrow window, a counter-bet that fills while the main bet is being closed elsewhere ends up live on the exchange — while the alert reports it flat. A naked counter-bet the bot swears it closed.

One entry's "defining flaw" from verdict one was overturned. The skeptic re-read the requirement and ruled it a documented, bounded design choice — not a defect. The penalty that had defined that entry's reputation was refunded.

And exactly one entry walked through every check: all eight required tests present, and every mutation killed. Its only deductions anywhere: two stale sentences in its own write-up.

⚖ Conflict of interest — read before believing the row above

The judge ran on Fable 5 — the orchestrator, all fifteen panel reviewers, all five skeptics — and Fable 5's own entry just won.

Weigh that seriously. Here's why the result is checkable anyway: reviewers saw PR numbers only — no model names, no attribution, descriptions redacted. The score sheet is arithmetic, not vibes. And every score-moving verdict is backed by a break-it-on-purpose mutation result anyone can re-run: the winner's margin comes from tests that demonstrably catch sabotage, and the fallers' losses come from tests that demonstrably don't. Also on the record: the same judge's first pass ranked its own entry second.

Same five entries, same code, two honest verdicts — different winners. The first pass paid for convincing stories; the second paid only for what it could break and watch fail. Fifteen and a half points now separate first from last, and almost the entire field changed places.
